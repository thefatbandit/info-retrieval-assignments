Theresa Chen

And I guess, given other industry participants expectations of structural changes in the market, you've seen closures of specific refining facilities in some areas that your assets touch, including New Mexico and Cheyenne, Wyoming. Michael Mears

Theresa Chen

I guess, touching on the tariffs point that you mentioned earlier about, higher tariffs from that movement, just in general, for the FERC controlled or FERC dictated tariffs portion of your refined product system, as we think about the next FERC escalator taking effect July 1, 2021, where do things currently stand given your role under AOPL? Do you have a view on where the outcome could shake out versus the starting point of 0.9%? Do you have an update there?

Michael Mears

I can tell you where AOPL's position and really the industry's position is and kind of frame the potential outcomes. I'm not in a position to predict what the commission will actually do. But I think we can frame the outcomes here. The commission has proposed a point 0.09% adder. There's two fundamental assumptions that underlie that calculation. One is how the commission is going to treat the MLP income tax policy that changed in the middle of this review cycle. And the second is what portion of the data set that the commission is going to consider.

With regards to the MLP policy change, the commission in their initial proposal has proposed not changing the data whatsoever. AOPL has proposed modifying the data to represent the fact that even though the policy changed, MLPs had no actual cost change associated with that policy change. They didn't pay a tax before the policy change, they aren't paying a tax after the policy change. So, that should be a neutral calculation in the index. So, that's one difference in the assumptions.

The other difference is the commission used the middle 50%. AOPL is arguing for the middle 80% because it's more representative of the actual cost experience of the industry. If you use the AOPL assumptions, the adder is 0.79% instead of 0.09%.

So, that's the likely outcome, is it's going to be likely somewhere in between those two numbers. Which arguments the commission will consider and which they won't, we don't know yet and we won't know till they actually make a decision, but that's the bounds of where the number is likely going to land.

Theresa Chen

Michael Mears

So, that's when we would expect it is – I can't be more precise that. Sometime between Friday and end of the year.

Theresa Chen

I guess maybe turning to your butane blending business within refined products, so given the volatility in both commodity prices and volumes this year, and I understand that you have been able to capture some of the [indiscernible] at the fall and executed those hedges already. Do you think it will still be like a sizable component of your cash flows as in 10% plus as you have done in previous years? Or with this flat price environment and the uncertainty in how the demand picture shakes out, could it shrink as a percentage of your total cash flows? What are your thoughts there?

Michael Mears

We don't expect a major structural shift in the blending business based on the COVID experience. The blending business is a true margin business. We blend butane into gasoline which creates more gasoline and capture the incremental margin. There's nothing that's happened to change that basic fact that butane can be blended into gasoline.

But the margin historically is loosely correlated with the absolute price of crude oil, with gasoline and butane kind of moving in tandem. The margin being much wider when crude oil is higher and lower when crude oil is lower. We don't expect that to change.

Now, obviously, we're in a low price environment. So, margins are relatively low right now. If crude oil gets back to $75 a barrel, for instance, then we would expect margins to be higher. I'm not making any crude oil price projections right now. But it is a cyclical business. We would expect these margins to be cyclical. And we would expect that structural relationship to change.

On the volume basis, the amount we can blend is dependent on the volume of gasoline that moves through the system. Again, we were optimistic and we don't have any reason to believe at this point we're not going to ultimately get back to a full recovery on gasoline demand. It's just a matter of how long. And so, we don't expect any structural change or problem there.
